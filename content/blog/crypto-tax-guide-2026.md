Crypto taxes are not optional. The IRS, HMRC, CRA, and tax authorities worldwide have made clear that cryptocurrency is a taxable asset — and increased reporting requirements mean authorities have more visibility into crypto transactions than ever before. Exchanges now issue 1099 forms. Blockchain analytics firms help agencies trace transactions.

Getting your crypto taxes right protects you from audits, penalties, and interest charges that can far exceed the original tax bill. This guide covers the essentials for U.S. taxpayers, with notes on other jurisdictions.

## The Fundamental Principle: Crypto Is Property

In the United States, the IRS classifies cryptocurrency as property, not currency. This means:

- Every time you sell, trade, or spend crypto, you trigger a taxable event
- Gains are calculated as: Sale Price - Cost Basis = Taxable Gain or Loss
- Gains held over 12 months qualify for lower long-term capital gains rates
- Gains held under 12 months are taxed as ordinary income

This treatment applies regardless of whether you convert to dollars or trade one crypto for another. BTC → ETH is a taxable event.

## Taxable Events

| Event | Taxable? | Type |
|-------|---------|------|
| Sell crypto for USD | Yes | Capital gain/loss |
| Trade crypto for crypto | Yes | Capital gain/loss |
| Spend crypto on goods/services | Yes | Capital gain/loss |
| Receive crypto as payment | Yes | Ordinary income |
| Mining rewards | Yes | Ordinary income |

## Capital Gains Tax Rates (U.S. 2026)

### Short-Term Capital Gains (held ≤ 12 months)

Taxed as ordinary income at your marginal rate:
- 10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, or 37% depending on taxable income bracket

### Long-Term Capital Gains (held > 12 months)

| Filing Status | 0% Rate | 15% Rate | 20% Rate |
|--------------|---------|---------|---------|
| Single | Up to $47,025 | $47,025–$518,900 | Above $518,900 |
| Married Filing Jointly | Up to $94,050 | $94,050–$583,750 | Above $583,750 |

*Figures are approximate for 2026. Verify current brackets with the IRS or a tax professional.*

**The most important tax strategy:** Hold assets for over 12 months to qualify for long-term rates. The difference between 37% (short-term) and 20% (long-term) is enormous over large gains.

## Staking and DeFi Yield Taxation

### Staking Rewards

The IRS issued guidance confirming staking rewards are taxable as ordinary income in the year received, at the fair market value on the date of receipt. If you stake ETH and receive 0.01 ETH when ETH is $3,000, you have $30 of ordinary income — even if you did not sell.

### DeFi Yield and Interest

DeFi interest (e.g., Aave supply interest, Curve LP fees) is generally treated as ordinary income when received. Rebase tokens like stETH that increase your balance daily technically trigger income recognition on each increase, though practical enforcement of daily micro-amounts is evolving.

### LP Token Considerations

When you provide liquidity to a DEX:
- Depositing tokens for LP tokens may be a taxable disposition in some interpretations
- Receiving trading fees is income
- Withdrawing from the pool is a taxable event on any appreciation of the underlying tokens
- Impermanent loss is generally recognized as a capital loss at withdrawal

DeFi tax treatment is complex and evolving. Consulting a DeFi-specialized CPA is strongly recommended for significant DeFi positions.

## Record Keeping Requirements

The IRS requires you to maintain records of:
- Date acquired
- Cost basis (purchase price in USD + fees)
- Date sold or disposed
- Sale proceeds
- Wallet addresses (for tracing purposes)

**Cost basis methods** (you choose, but must be consistent):
- **FIFO (First In, First Out):** Most common; oldest coins sold first
- **HIFO (Highest In, First Out):** Minimizes gains by selling highest-cost basis first
- **Specific Identification:** Choose which lot to sell; requires detailed records

HIFO typically minimizes tax liability but requires meticulous per-transaction tracking.

## Tax-Loss Harvesting

Tax-loss harvesting means selling losing positions to realize capital losses that offset your gains. Key point: **crypto does not have a wash sale rule** (as of 2026). Stocks have a 30-day wash sale period preventing you from immediately repurchasing a sold security. Crypto does not — you can sell BTC at a loss and immediately buy it back, crystallizing the loss for tax purposes while maintaining your position.

**Example:**
- You bought 1 ETH at $4,000 (current value $2,500). Unrealized loss: $1,500.
- You sell: realize $1,500 capital loss
- Immediately buy 1 ETH back at $2,500
- New cost basis: $2,500
- The $1,500 loss offsets other gains or up to $3,000 of ordinary income

Year-end tax-loss harvesting (October–December) is one of the highest-value activities for active crypto investors.

## International Notes

**UK:** HMRC treats crypto as a capital asset; annual exempt amount of £3,000. Staking rewards are income at receipt. Bed and breakfasting rules prevent same-day or next-day repurchases from crystallizing losses.

**Canada:** CRA treats crypto as a commodity; 50% of capital gains are included in income.

**Germany:** Crypto held over 12 months is tax-free. Staking may reset the holding period.

**Australia:** ATO treats crypto as property; personal use asset exemption for small amounts.

Tax laws change. Follow [Crypto Vision News](/) and consult a tax professional annually to stay current.

## Action Plan for Tax Season

1. **Export all transaction history** from every exchange and wallet used
2. **Import into tax software** (Koinly, CoinTracker, etc.)
3. **Reconcile DeFi transactions** manually if needed — tax software often struggles with complex DeFi interactions
4. **Review harvesting opportunities** before December 31
5. **Consult a crypto-specialized CPA** if you have significant DeFi activity, mining income, or complex situations
6. **File Form 8949** for capital gains/losses; Schedule D summary; Schedule 1 for income

The penalty for negligence can be 20% of underpaid tax. Willful failure to report can result in criminal prosecution. Take your crypto taxes seriously.
