In March 2019, an anonymous Dutch analyst who goes by "Plan B" published a paper proposing that Bitcoin's price could be modeled based on its stock-to-flow ratio — a measure of scarcity borrowed from commodity economics. The model attracted enormous attention, generated aggressive predictions, and sparked a debate that continues in 2026. Let's examine the evidence objectively.

## What Is Stock-to-Flow?

Stock-to-flow (S2F) is a ratio used to measure the scarcity of commodities. It was originally applied to precious metals:

- **Stock:** The total existing above-ground supply of a commodity
- **Flow:** The annual new production of that commodity

**S2F Ratio = Stock / Flow**

A high S2F ratio means very little new supply is being added relative to existing supply — indicating high scarcity. Gold has an S2F ratio of around 60 (60 years of current mining production would be needed to double the existing supply). Silver is around 22. Bitcoin's S2F increases with each halving.

## Bitcoin's S2F and Halvings

Bitcoin's supply model is uniquely suited to S2F analysis:

- Total supply is hard-capped at 21 million BTC
- New supply is created through mining, but the reward halves every 210,000 blocks (approximately every 4 years)
- Each halving dramatically reduces "flow" while "stock" continues to accumulate

Before the 2020 halving, Bitcoin's S2F was around 27. After the 2020 halving, it jumped to ~56, comparable to gold. After the 2024 halving (the fourth halving), Bitcoin's S2F exceeded 100, making it the scarcest asset on Earth by this measure — scarcer than gold.

## Plan B's Model and Predictions

Plan B's original 2019 paper showed a strong statistical correlation between Bitcoin's S2F ratio and its price over the previous 8 years of data. The relationship appeared log-linear: each time the S2F ratio doubled, price roughly increased by a factor of 10.

Based on this relationship, Plan B predicted:
- Post-2020 halving: Bitcoin would reach ~$100,000
- Post-2024 halving: Bitcoin would reach $500,000 or higher

He later extended the model to "S2F Cross Asset" (S2FX), which included gold and silver as data points and suggested even higher price targets.

The 2020-2021 predictions attracted enormous attention when Bitcoin did reach $69,000 in November 2021 — within the model's predicted range, though it subsequently collapsed to $15,500 in 2022, far below the model's "minimum" floor.

## How Have the Predictions Held Up?

This is where honest assessment is required.

**What worked:** The model correctly directionally predicted that Bitcoin would reach very high prices following the 2020 halving, and the general magnitude was in the right ballpark for the 2021 peak.

**What failed:** The model projected price floors that Bitcoin violated catastrophically. In 2022, Bitcoin traded far below what S2F suggested was a minimum supportable price. During the 2022 bear market, Plan B revised interpretations and timelines multiple times, which critics noted as unfalsifiable behavior — changing the model when it fails.

**Post-2024 halving:** Bitcoin did achieve significant all-time highs following the April 2024 halving, consistent with S2F's directional prediction. However, prices remained within ranges that could be explained by ETF demand, broader risk-on markets, and other factors — making it difficult to attribute the price action specifically to S2F dynamics.

## Statistical Criticisms

Professional statisticians and economists have published serious criticisms of the S2F model:

### Data Mining and Overfitting

Critics point out that Plan B's model was constructed by fitting a curve to Bitcoin's historical price data. With enough parameters, you can fit almost any trend to historical data. The question is whether the model has genuine predictive power out-of-sample.

### Small Sample Size

Bitcoin has only had two halvings before the analysis was published. Drawing strong statistical conclusions from two data points is methodologically problematic.

### Non-Stationarity

Stock prices and commodity prices are non-stationary time series — they have unit roots. Standard regression analysis on non-stationary time series can produce spurious correlations that appear strong but have no causal relationship. The apparent correlation between S2F and Bitcoin's price may partly be statistical artifact.

### Causality vs. Correlation

Even if the correlation is real, the causal mechanism is debated. Does Bitcoin's price increase because the S2F ratio increased? Or are both S2F and price driven by Bitcoin's broader adoption and market development, making the correlation coincidental rather than causal?

### What the Model Misses

S2F measures supply-side scarcity but says nothing about:
- Demand (which drives price alongside supply)
- Regulatory environments
- Competition from other assets
- Macroeconomic conditions
- Market cycles and sentiment

Bitcoin's price in 2022 wasn't affected by any change in its S2F ratio — flow was exactly as expected. The crash was entirely demand-driven, something S2F cannot model.

## The Scarcity Narrative's Real Value

Even if S2F's specific quantitative predictions are unreliable, the underlying scarcity narrative captures something real:

Bitcoin is genuinely the most scarce large financial asset ever created. Its supply schedule is algorithmically determined and cannot be changed without near-unanimous network consensus. Halvings mechanically reduce new supply, creating at minimum a supply shock that interacts with demand.

These are fundamental Bitcoin properties that genuinely distinguish it from every fiat currency and most other assets. The question is whether this scarcity mechanically translates to price appreciation at specific levels, or whether it simply provides a long-term tailwind as a value proposition.

## How to Use S2F Wisely

S2F is most useful as:

**A relative framework:** Bitcoin's S2F exceeding gold's is a genuinely meaningful milestone for comparing monetary properties of assets.

**A bull market sentiment indicator:** When prices deviate far below the S2F model line, it often (not always) indicates extreme pessimism. When prices deviate far above, it may indicate mania.

**One input among many:** Combined with on-chain analytics (realized price, MVRV ratio, SOPR), macro analysis, and technical analysis, S2F contributes context to Bitcoin price assessment.

**Not as a price oracle:** Using S2F to set price targets with high confidence or to make leveraged bets has caused significant losses for investors who treated it as a reliable prediction.

The sophisticated view in 2026: Bitcoin's scarcity is real and matters. The specific quantitative price model built on that scarcity is useful for illustration and context, not for precise price prediction. Treat it accordingly.
