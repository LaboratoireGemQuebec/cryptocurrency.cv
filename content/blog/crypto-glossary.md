Whether you're new to crypto or a seasoned trader, this glossary covers the 100 most essential terms in the cryptocurrency ecosystem. Bookmark it as your reference for any term you encounter in news, social media, or protocol documentation.

---

## A

**ASIC (Application-Specific Integrated Circuit)**
A specialized computer chip designed specifically for cryptocurrency mining. Bitcoin ASICs are orders of magnitude more efficient at Bitcoin's SHA-256 hashing algorithm than general-purpose CPUs or GPUs. ASICs have made Bitcoin mining highly competitive and energy-intensive.

**ATH (All-Time High)**
The highest price a cryptocurrency has ever reached. "BTC hit a new ATH" means Bitcoin reached a new record price. Useful reference point for market sentiment analysis.

**ATL (All-Time Low)**
The lowest price a cryptocurrency has ever reached. Less commonly discussed than ATH, but relevant for evaluating how far above bottom a coin currently trades.

---

## B

**BIP (Bitcoin Improvement Proposal)**
The formal process for proposing changes to the Bitcoin protocol. BIPs are numbered documents (BIP-32 defined HD wallets, BIP-141 was SegWit, BIP-341 was Taproot) that are debated by the community before potential adoption. Similar to Ethereum's EIPs.

**Block**
The basic unit of a blockchain. A block contains a batch of validated transactions, a timestamp, reference to the previous block (creating the "chain"), and a cryptographic proof (PoW hash or PoS signature). Bitcoin produces a new block approximately every 10 minutes.

**Blockchain**
A distributed ledger where records (blocks) are linked cryptographically in chronological order. Once written, blocks cannot be altered without redoing all subsequent work — the property that makes blockchains secure and tamper-evident.

**Bridge**
A protocol that enables transfer of assets or data between separate blockchain networks. Bridges lock assets on the source chain and mint equivalent representations on the destination chain. They have been the most hacked category of crypto infrastructure.

**Burned Tokens**
Tokens permanently removed from circulation by sending to an unspendable address (a "burn address"). Token burns reduce total supply over time, potentially increasing scarcity. Many protocols burn tokens from fees or via buy-and-burn mechanisms.

---

## C

**CEX (Centralized Exchange)**
A cryptocurrency exchange operated by a company that holds customer funds in custody. Examples: Coinbase, Binance, Kraken. Contrasted with DEX (decentralized exchange). CEXs require KYC/AML compliance and are subject to regulatory requirements.

**CeFi (Centralized Finance)**
Financial services for cryptocurrency that use centralized institutions — exchanges, custodians, lending platforms. Contrasted with DeFi. CeFi provides simpler user experience but involves counterparty risk (see: FTX, Celsius collapses).

**Cold Wallet (Cold Storage)**
A cryptocurrency wallet not connected to the internet. Hardware wallets (Ledger, Trezor) and paper wallets are cold storage methods. Cold wallets are the most secure way to store cryptocurrency long-term.

**Consensus**
The mechanism by which all nodes in a blockchain network agree on the current state of the ledger. Bitcoin uses Proof of Work consensus; Ethereum uses Proof of Stake. Consensus ensures all participants agree on which transactions are valid.

---

## D

**DAO (Decentralized Autonomous Organization)**
An organization governed by smart contracts and token holder votes rather than traditional corporate hierarchy. Token holders propose and vote on decisions; approved proposals execute automatically. MakerDAO, Uniswap, and Compound are major DAOs.

**DApp (Decentralized Application)**
An application built on a blockchain whose core logic runs in smart contracts rather than on centralized servers. DApps are permissionless and censorship-resistant. Uniswap, Aave, and OpenSea are DApps.

**DeFi (Decentralized Finance)**
Financial services — lending, borrowing, trading, derivatives — built on public blockchains using smart contracts, accessible to anyone with a wallet. DeFi operates without banks or intermediaries and processes billions of dollars in transactions daily.

**DEX (Decentralized Exchange)**
A cryptocurrency exchange that operates via smart contracts rather than a central company. Users trade directly from their wallets without depositing funds on the exchange. Uniswap, Curve, and dYdX are DEXs.

**Deflation**
A decrease in the total money supply of a cryptocurrency over time, typically via token burns. Deflationary assets may increase in value as supply decreases. Contrasted with inflation (increasing supply).

---

## E

**ERC-20**
The standard technical specification for fungible tokens on the Ethereum blockchain. Most Ethereum-based tokens follow ERC-20, making them compatible with wallets, exchanges, and DeFi protocols. ERC-721 is the standard for NFTs; ERC-1155 allows both fungible and non-fungible tokens.

**EVM (Ethereum Virtual Machine)**
The computation engine that executes smart contracts on Ethereum. The EVM is sandboxed — contract execution is isolated from the underlying network. Many other blockchains (BNB Chain, Polygon, Avalanche C-Chain) are "EVM-compatible," meaning Ethereum smart contracts can run on them.

---

## F

**Fiat**
Government-issued currency not backed by a physical commodity. USD, EUR, JPY are fiat currencies. In crypto contexts, "fiat" refers to traditional currency. "Fiat off-ramp" means converting crypto back to traditional currency.

**Fork (Hard Fork)**
A blockchain change that is backward-incompatible — all nodes must upgrade to the new rules. If the community splits on whether to adopt the change, two separate chains can result (as happened when Ethereum Classic split from Ethereum after The DAO hack).

**Fork (Soft Fork)**
A backward-compatible blockchain upgrade where new rules are a subset of old rules. Nodes running old software can still validate blocks from upgraded nodes, though they won't enforce new rules. Bitcoin's SegWit (BIP-141) was a soft fork.

**FUD (Fear, Uncertainty, Doubt)**
Negative news or sentiment, often spread intentionally to drive price down. "FUD" can describe both genuine concerns and deliberate misinformation campaigns. Contrasted with FOMO.

**FOMO (Fear of Missing Out)**
The anxiety of missing a profitable opportunity, often driving irrational buying at market peaks. FOMO-driven purchases near ATHs have caused significant losses for retail investors.

---

## G

**Gas**
The unit measuring computational work required to execute operations on Ethereum. Every smart contract function, token transfer, and on-chain interaction requires gas. Gas fees are paid in ETH (denominated in Gwei) to compensate validators.

**Gwei**
One billionth of an ETH (10^-9 ETH). Gas prices are typically quoted in Gwei. A gas price of 20 Gwei means you pay 20/1,000,000,000 ETH per unit of gas consumed.

---

## H

**Halving**
A scheduled reduction in Bitcoin's block reward. Every 210,000 blocks (~4 years), the reward miners receive per block is cut in half. This reduces new BTC issuance. Halvings in 2012, 2016, 2020, and 2024 have historically preceded bull markets. The mechanism enforces Bitcoin's disinflationary supply schedule.

**Hash Rate**
The total computational power being used to mine or process transactions on a proof-of-work blockchain. Higher hash rate means greater network security (more work needed to attack). Bitcoin's hash rate is measured in exahashes per second (EH/s).

**HODL**
A misspelling of "hold" that became crypto slang for holding crypto through price volatility rather than selling. Originates from a 2013 BitcoinTalk forum post. "HODL" is now the dominant term for long-term holding strategy.

---

## I

**ICO (Initial Coin Offering)**
A fundraising method where new crypto projects sell tokens to early investors in exchange for ETH or BTC. ICOs were popular in 2017-2018 but largely replaced by IEOs, IDOs, and other mechanisms following regulatory crackdowns.

**IDO (Initial DEX Offering)**
Token launch directly on a decentralized exchange. IDOs allow projects to launch tokens permissionlessly without exchange listing fees or centralized gatekeepers. Common on Uniswap and Raydium.

**Impermanent Loss (IL)**
The temporary loss experienced by liquidity providers when the price ratio of their deposited asset pair changes. If you deposit ETH/USDC and ETH doubles, you end up with less ETH (and more USDC) than if you had simply held ETH. The loss is "impermanent" because it reverses if prices return to the original ratio. IL can make LP positions less profitable than simply holding.

---

## L

**L1 / L2 (Layer 1 / Layer 2)**
Layer 1 is the base blockchain (Bitcoin, Ethereum, Solana). Layer 2 is a system built on top of L1 that processes transactions off-chain and periodically settles on L1, achieving higher throughput and lower fees. Lightning Network (Bitcoin L2) and Arbitrum (Ethereum L2) are examples.

**Liquidity Pool**
A smart contract holding paired assets (e.g., ETH + USDC) that enables decentralized trading on AMM DEXs. Liquidity providers deposit assets and earn trading fees. Pool depth determines price impact of trades.

**LP Token**
A token received when providing liquidity to a pool, representing your share of the pool's assets. LP tokens can be redeemed for the underlying assets plus earned fees. LP tokens are also used in yield farming to earn additional rewards.

---

## M

**Market Cap (Market Capitalization)**
Price × circulating supply. The total value of all tokens currently in circulation. Bitcoin's market cap exceeds $1 trillion as of 2026. Market cap is a more accurate measure of network size than price alone.

**MEV (Maximal Extractable Value)**
Profit extracted by manipulating the order of transactions in a blockchain block. Types include frontrunning, sandwich attacks, and arbitrage. MEV is an invisible tax on DeFi users that sophisticated bots capture.

**Mining**
The process of validating transactions on a proof-of-work blockchain by repeatedly hashing block data to find a valid hash below a target value. Successful miners earn block rewards and transaction fees. Requires significant computational power and electricity.

**Multisig (Multi-signature)**
A security feature requiring multiple private keys to authorize a transaction. A "3-of-5 multisig" requires 3 of 5 designated key holders to sign. Used for treasury management, smart contract upgrades, and secure self-custody.

---

## N

**NFT (Non-Fungible Token)**
A unique token representing ownership of a specific asset — digital art, music, game items, or real-world assets. Unlike fungible tokens (where each token is identical), each NFT has a unique identifier and associated metadata. NFT ownership is recorded on-chain.

**Node**
A computer that participates in a blockchain network by downloading and validating the blockchain. Full nodes verify all transactions against protocol rules. Mining/validator nodes also produce new blocks. Running a full node is the highest-trust way to use Bitcoin or Ethereum.

---

## O

**On-chain**
Activity that is recorded directly on the blockchain as a transaction. Contrasted with "off-chain" activity (like order books on centralized exchanges) which doesn't create a blockchain record.

**Oracle**
A service that provides external data (like price feeds) to smart contracts. Blockchains cannot access off-chain data natively; oracles bridge this gap. Chainlink is the dominant decentralized oracle network. Oracle manipulation is a significant DeFi attack vector.

---

## P

**Permissionless**
The property of being accessible to anyone without requiring approval from a gatekeeper. Bitcoin and Ethereum are permissionless — anyone can participate without KYC, applications, or geographic restrictions.

**Private Key**
A secret cryptographic number that proves ownership of a blockchain address and authorizes transactions. Never share your private key. If someone else has your private key, they can steal your funds. Often represented as a 12-24 word seed phrase.

**PoW (Proof of Work)**
The consensus mechanism used by Bitcoin. Miners compete to find a hash solution that meets the network's difficulty target, expending computational energy. The winner adds the next block and earns the block reward. PoW makes attacks expensive because they require enormous computational resources.

**PoS (Proof of Stake)**
The consensus mechanism used by Ethereum post-Merge. Validators lock (stake) cryptocurrency as collateral and are selected to validate blocks proportional to their stake. Dishonest validators lose their stake ("slashing"). PoS is more energy-efficient than PoW.

**Protocol**
In crypto, a set of rules defining how a blockchain or DeFi system operates. "The Uniswap protocol" refers to the smart contract logic governing Uniswap's decentralized exchange. Protocols are typically open-source and run autonomously.

**Public Key**
A cryptographic key derived from your private key that others can use to verify your signatures and generate your wallet address. Your wallet address is derived from your public key. Sharing your public key or address is safe; sharing your private key is not.

---

## R

**Rug Pull**
A type of crypto scam where developers abandon a project and drain its liquidity after attracting investor funds. The "rug is pulled" from under investors. Rug pulls are especially common in meme coins and anonymous DeFi projects.

---

## S

**Seed Phrase (Recovery Phrase)**
A human-readable backup of your wallet's private keys, typically 12 or 24 words. Your seed phrase can regenerate all accounts in your wallet. Store it offline, never digitally. Anyone with your seed phrase has complete access to your funds.

**Smart Contract**
Self-executing code deployed on a blockchain that automatically enforces the terms of an agreement. Smart contracts run exactly as programmed, with no possibility of downtime, censorship, or third-party interference. The foundation of DeFi.

**Stablecoin**
A cryptocurrency designed to maintain a stable value, typically pegged to the US dollar. Types include fiat-backed (USDT, USDC), crypto-backed (DAI), and algorithmic (largely failed after UST's collapse). Essential infrastructure for DeFi.

**Staking**
Locking cryptocurrency in a protocol to earn rewards. Ethereum validators stake 32 ETH to participate in consensus and earn rewards. DeFi protocols offer staking for governance participation or yield. Staking typically involves lockup periods.

---

## T

**TVL (Total Value Locked)**
The total dollar value of assets deposited in a DeFi protocol. TVL is the primary metric for comparing DeFi protocol size. Tracked by DeFiLlama across all chains and protocols.

**Tokenomics**
The economic design of a cryptocurrency — supply, distribution, emission schedule, use cases, and incentive structures. Well-designed tokenomics create sustainable ecosystems; poorly designed tokenomics cause token collapses.

**TPS (Transactions Per Second)**
A measure of blockchain throughput. Bitcoin: ~7 TPS. Ethereum L1: ~15-30 TPS. Solana: ~65,000 theoretical TPS. Layer 2 solutions significantly increase effective throughput.

---

## U

**USDC**
USD Coin, a fully-reserved fiat-backed stablecoin issued by Circle. USDC is considered the most transparent and regulated major stablecoin, with monthly attestation reports confirming 1:1 dollar backing.

---

## V

**Validator**
A node participant in a proof-of-stake network that stakes cryptocurrency as collateral and processes/validates transactions. Validators earn rewards for honest participation and face slashing for misbehavior. Ethereum requires 32 ETH to become a solo validator.

**Vesting**
A schedule that gradually releases tokens over time. Used for team and investor token allocations to prevent immediate selling after a token launch. A "4-year vesting with 1-year cliff" means no tokens are released for the first year, then they vest monthly over 3 more years.

---

## W

**Wallet**
Software or hardware that stores private keys and allows users to manage cryptocurrency. Wallets don't actually hold coins — coins live on the blockchain. The wallet holds the keys that prove ownership. Types include hot wallets (internet-connected), cold wallets (offline), and hardware wallets.

**Whitepaper**
A technical document published by a cryptocurrency or blockchain project describing its design, purpose, and mechanics. Bitcoin's whitepaper (2008) by Satoshi Nakamoto is the foundational document of the entire industry.

---

## Y

**Yield Farming**
The practice of moving cryptocurrency between DeFi protocols to maximize returns. Yield farmers deposit assets where incentive rewards are highest, sometimes using complex strategies with staking, liquidity provision, and borrowing. High potential returns come with high smart contract risk.

---

## Z

**ZK Proof (Zero-Knowledge Proof)**
A cryptographic method allowing one party to prove knowledge of information to another without revealing the information itself. ZK proofs are the foundation of zkRollups (Ethereum scaling) and privacy coins (Zcash). Two major types: zk-SNARKs (compact but requiring trusted setup) and zk-STARKs (no trusted setup, post-quantum secure).

**ZK Rollup**
A Layer 2 scaling solution that batches thousands of transactions off-chain and submits a zero-knowledge proof of their validity to the main chain. ZK rollups (zkSync, StarkNet, Polygon zkEVM) achieve high throughput while inheriting Ethereum's security. Considered the most promising long-term Ethereum scaling solution.
