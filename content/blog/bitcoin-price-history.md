Bitcoin has gone from a curiosity worth fractions of a cent to a globally traded asset worth over $100,000. This journey spans nearly two decades, four halvings, multiple boom-and-bust cycles, regulatory battles, exchange collapses, and eventual institutional acceptance. Here is the complete story.

## 2009: The Genesis Block

On January 3, 2009, Satoshi Nakamoto mined the first Bitcoin block — the Genesis Block — embedding the headline "The Times 03/Jan/2009 Chancellor on brink of second bailout for banks." This message served as both a timestamp and a philosophical declaration about Bitcoin's purpose.

For most of 2009, Bitcoin had no market price. It was mined and exchanged for free between cryptography enthusiasts on forums. The first documented transaction — 10,000 BTC for two pizzas (the famous "Bitcoin Pizza Day," May 22, 2010) — implied a price of roughly **$0.0025 per BTC**.

## 2010–2011: The First Price Discovery

Bitcoin's first exchange, Mt. Gox, launched in July 2010. By the end of 2010, Bitcoin was trading at **$0.30**. In early 2011, it reached **$1** for the first time — a milestone celebrated by early adopters.

The first major bubble arrived in June 2011 when Bitcoin hit **$31** before crashing back to $2 by November 2011. This 90%+ decline was the first of many Bitcoin "deaths" that turned out to be temporary.

## 2013: Breaking $1,000 for the First Time

2013 saw Bitcoin's first mainstream moment. Two separate bubbles occurred:

- **April 2013**: Bitcoin surged to **$266** before crashing to $50
- **November 2013**: Bitcoin broke through **$1,000** for the first time, driven by growing mainstream awareness, Chinese demand, and the Silk Road narrative

The year ended with Bitcoin around $750 as China's central bank restricted financial institutions from transacting in Bitcoin.

## 2014–2015: The Mt. Gox Collapse and Bear Market

In February 2014, Mt. Gox — then handling 70% of all Bitcoin transactions — announced it had been hacked and lost **850,000 BTC** (worth ~$450 million at the time). This remains one of the largest crypto catastrophes in history.

Bitcoin fell from ~$1,000 to under **$200** by early 2015. The next two years were a prolonged bear market that tested the resolve of even the most committed holders.

## 2016–2017: ICO Boom and $20,000

The second halving in July 2016 planted the seeds of the next bull run. But the real catalyst was the **Initial Coin Offering (ICO) craze** on Ethereum, which brought millions of new users and capital into the space.

The launch of Bitcoin futures on the CME in December 2017 coincided almost exactly with the peak. Retail euphoria was at maximum levels.

## 2018: The Crypto Winter

2018 saw Bitcoin decline from ~$17,000 at the start of the year to **$3,150 by December** — a drawdown of over 83%. Most altcoins lost 90–99% of their value. The ICO bubble had burst, regulators were cracking down, and the "blockchain not Bitcoin" narrative was fading.

## 2019–2020: Recovery and Pre-Halving Accumulation

Bitcoin recovered to ~$13,000 in June 2019 before settling back to $7,000. The March 2020 COVID-19 market crash briefly pushed Bitcoin below **$4,000** — its lowest point since 2019 — before an extraordinary recovery began.

By the time of the third halving in May 2020, Bitcoin had recovered to ~$8,600. What followed was the most institutionally-driven bull market in Bitcoin's history.

## 2020–2021: Institutional Adoption and New All-Time Highs

The 2020–2021 cycle was different from all previous cycles. For the first time, major corporations and institutions began accumulating Bitcoin:

- **MicroStrategy** (August 2020): Began purchasing Bitcoin as a primary treasury asset, eventually accumulating over 100,000 BTC
- **Square** (now Block): Purchased $50M in Bitcoin
- **Tesla** (February 2021): Purchased $1.5B in Bitcoin, briefly accepted it as payment
- **PayPal**: Enabled Bitcoin buying for 350M users
- **Grayscale**: Bitcoin Trust grew to hold $30B+ in BTC

## 2022: FTX Collapse and Crypto Winter 2.0

2022 was catastrophic:

- **May 2022**: The Terra/LUNA collapse wiped out $40B+ in market cap overnight, triggering contagion across the market
- **June 2022**: Bitcoin fell below $20,000 (the previous ATH), a psychological disaster
- **November 2022**: FTX, the world's second-largest crypto exchange, collapsed in days amid fraud allegations. CEO Sam Bankman-Fried was later convicted of multiple fraud charges.

Bitcoin bottomed at approximately **$15,500 in November 2022**.

## 2023: The Quiet Recovery

2023 began with Bitcoin near its lows but saw a steady accumulation phase. Key catalysts:

- BlackRock's Bitcoin ETF application (June 2023) signaled institutional confidence
- Bitcoin's resilience compared to the broader "crypto contagion" narrative
- Growing regulatory clarity in the US

Bitcoin ended 2023 near **$42,000** — nearly tripling from its November 2022 lows.

## 2024: ETF Approval and the Fourth Halving

January 2024 brought the approval of spot Bitcoin ETFs in the US. The next six months saw:

- Bitcoin ETFs attracting **$10B+ in inflows** within the first quarter
- Bitcoin breaking its 2021 all-time high of $69,000 in March 2024
- The fourth halving in April 2024, reducing the block reward to 3.125 BTC
- Bitcoin reaching **$108,000+** by year-end 2024

## 2025–2026: The Current Era

By early 2026, Bitcoin has established itself as a mainstream asset class:

- Spot Bitcoin ETFs hold **hundreds of billions** in AUM
- Multiple sovereign nations hold Bitcoin as a reserve asset
- Bitcoin's market cap rivals major stock market components
- The fourth halving's supply shock continues to reverberate

## Bitcoin's Four-Year Cycle Pattern

The historical pattern is remarkably consistent:

1. Halving occurs (supply shock)
2. Bitcoin price rises 12–18 months later (supply absorption)
3. Parabolic blow-off top occurs
4. 80%+ bear market drawdown follows
5. Recovery and accumulation begin
6. Next halving approaches

Whether this cycle continues exactly as before is debatable — institutional involvement, spot ETFs, and macro factors may dampen both the peaks and the troughs. But the underlying supply schedule remains unchanged.

## Conclusion

Bitcoin's price history is a record of an asset moving from mathematical curiosity to global financial instrument in under two decades. Each cycle has been larger, attracted more institutional participation, and ended at a higher "base" level than the previous. The story is far from over.
