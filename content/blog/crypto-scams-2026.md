Cryptocurrency scams cost victims over $5.6 billion in 2023 alone, according to the FBI. In 2026, the numbers are worse — AI-generated content, deepfake videos, and increasingly sophisticated social engineering have made scams harder to detect than ever. The good news is that most scams share recognizable patterns. Knowing them can save your portfolio.

Here are the eight most prevalent crypto scams and exactly how to identify and avoid them.

## 1. Rug Pulls

A rug pull happens when developers launch a token, attract investment, then drain the liquidity pool and disappear. The token price collapses to near zero instantly.

**How it works:** Developers create a token on a DEX (Uniswap, PancakeSwap), market it aggressively on Telegram and Twitter, and incentivize early buyers. Once enough liquidity accumulates, they withdraw everything and shut down communication.

**Red flags:** Anonymous team, no audit, vague whitepaper, locked liquidity with a short timer, pressure to buy quickly.

**How to check:** Use Token Sniffer or RugDoc to scan contracts before buying. Verify liquidity is locked for a meaningful period (years, not weeks).

## 2. Pig Butchering (Sha Zhu Pan)

Pig butchering is the most financially devastating scam of the decade. Victims are cultivated over weeks or months through a fake romantic or friendly relationship before being convinced to invest in a fraudulent platform.

**How it works:** A stranger contacts you on dating apps, social media, or via "wrong number" text. They build a genuine-seeming relationship over time, then casually mention they've been making money in crypto. They guide you to a fake investment platform that shows fabricated profits. You deposit more and more. When you try to withdraw, fees appear, then the platform vanishes.

**Red flags:** Unsolicited contact, too-good investment returns, pressure to keep investing, inability to withdraw funds.

**What to do:** If someone you've never met in person is giving you investment advice, stop contact immediately. No legitimate investment requires you to move money to a platform someone else recommended.

## 3. Fake Exchanges and Wallets

Scammers clone the interfaces of popular exchanges and wallets to capture login credentials and private keys.

**How it works:** A fake site mimics Binance, Coinbase, or MetaMask pixel-perfectly. Users enter their seed phrase or credentials, handing over full account access.

**Red flags:** Slightly misspelled domain names (bìnance.com, coinbàse.io), found through Google ads, pop-up requests for seed phrases.

**Protection:** Bookmark exchange URLs directly. Never click links in emails or messages. No legitimate service will ever ask for your seed phrase.

## 4. Ponzi and Pyramid Schemes

These promise guaranteed high returns — often 1-5% daily — funded by new investor deposits rather than legitimate trading.

**How it works:** Early investors receive payments that create social proof. The scheme collapses when recruitment slows and withdrawals exceed deposits. Bitconnect is the canonical example.

**Red flags:** Guaranteed returns, referral bonuses, vague or nonexistent trading strategy, pressure to recruit others.

## 5. Phishing Attacks

Phishing targets your credentials, seed phrases, or wallet approvals through deceptive links and fake websites.

**Types:** Email phishing mimicking exchanges, fake Discord DMs from "support," malicious Google ads, wallet approval requests that drain funds.

**Red flags:** Urgency ("your account will be suspended"), requests for seed phrases, approval requests with unlimited token spending amounts.

**Protection:** Use a hardware wallet. Regularly check and revoke token approvals at revoke.cash.

## 6. Fake Customer Support

Scammers monitor forums, Discord servers, and social media for users reporting problems. They reach out posing as official support staff.

**How it works:** You post about a transaction issue on Reddit or Telegram. Minutes later, a "support agent" messages you privately offering to help. They eventually ask for your seed phrase or remote access to your device.

**Critical rule:** No legitimate support agent ever needs your seed phrase or private key. Official support never contacts you first.

## 7. Romance Scams

Distinct from pig butchering in that the investment angle may come later and the emotional manipulation is deeper. Victims may send crypto directly as "gifts" or "emergency loans."

**Red flags:** Relationship entirely online, person always has reasons they can't video call clearly, requests for money in any form, escalating financial requests.

**Reality check:** If an online relationship — no matter how real it feels — involves any request for cryptocurrency, it is almost certainly a scam.

## 8. Fake Airdrops and Token Rewards

You receive tokens in your wallet claiming to be valuable. The scam involves getting you to approve a malicious contract to "claim" them.

**How it works:** Tokens appear in your wallet from an unknown source. The associated website asks you to connect and approve a transaction that actually grants the contract unlimited access to drain your real assets.

**Rule:** Never interact with unexpected tokens. Never approve transactions from unfamiliar contracts. Check any contract on Etherscan before approving.

## Universal Red Flags

Regardless of scam type, watch for these patterns:

- **Guaranteed returns** — no investment is risk-free
- **Urgency and pressure** — legitimate opportunities don't disappear in minutes
- **Unsolicited contact** — real opportunities don't find you randomly
- **Requests for seed phrases** — never, under any circumstances
- **Too good to be true** — it is

## What to Do If You've Been Scammed

1. **Stop all contact and transactions immediately.** Don't send more money hoping to recover losses.
2. **Document everything** — screenshots, transaction IDs, usernames, website URLs.
3. **Report to authorities:** FBI IC3 (ic3.gov) in the US, Action Fraud in the UK, or your national cybercrime unit.
4. **Report the platform** to the FTC, CFTC, or SEC if it involves securities.
5. **Warn others** on Reddit, Twitter, and relevant forums.

Be cautious of "recovery services" that promise to retrieve lost crypto — these are almost always secondary scams targeting victims who are already desperate.

The crypto space offers genuine opportunity, but scammers have professionalized their operations. Skepticism is not paranoia — it's required due diligence.
