NFTs (Non-Fungible Tokens) became one of the most talked-about — and misunderstood — concepts in crypto. At their peak in 2021-2022, they generated billions in trading volume and introduced mainstream audiences to blockchain technology. But what are they actually? This guide explains NFTs from first principles.

## Fungible vs. Non-Fungible

To understand NFTs, start with the concept of fungibility.

**Fungible** means interchangeable. One dollar is worth exactly the same as any other dollar. One Bitcoin equals one Bitcoin. It doesn't matter which specific Bitcoin you have — they're identical in value and utility. That's fungibility.

**Non-fungible** means unique and not directly interchangeable. A specific painting is non-fungible — one Mona Lisa does not equal any other painting. A plot of land is non-fungible — no two parcels are identical. A concert ticket for row A, seat 12 is non-fungible — it's for a specific seat at a specific time.

NFTs apply this concept to digital items. Before NFTs, digital files were infinitely copyable with no distinction between the "original" and copies. NFTs create a verifiable record of ownership and uniqueness on a blockchain.

## How NFTs Work Technically

NFTs are tokens on a blockchain that implement a standard ensuring each token has a unique identifier. On Ethereum, the primary standard is **ERC-721**, which defines a contract where each token has a unique `tokenId` and an owner address.

Here's the basic structure:

- **Smart contract:** A deployed contract following ERC-721 or ERC-1155 standards
- **Token ID:** A unique identifier within that contract (token #1, #2, #3, etc.)
- **Owner:** The current wallet address that holds the token
- **Metadata:** Usually a link to a JSON file describing the NFT (name, description, image URL, attributes)
- **Media:** The actual image, video, or file — typically stored on IPFS for decentralization

When you "buy an NFT," you're receiving ownership of a specific token ID in a specific contract, recorded on-chain. That ownership is publicly verifiable by anyone.

**What you actually own:** The token record and its associated metadata. The underlying image or file can technically be copied by anyone — what's unique is the token representing the "official" version, provenance, and ownership history.

## What Gives NFTs Value?

This is where it gets philosophical. Several factors contribute:

### Scarcity
A collection with only 10,000 items has enforced on-chain scarcity. The contract can only mint 10,000 tokens. This mirrors the concept of limited editions in physical art.

### Community and Status
Owning a CryptoPunk or Bored Ape was (and for some, still is) a social signal — membership in an exclusive community. Profile picture NFTs displayed ownership publicly on Twitter.

### Utility
Gaming NFTs represent in-game assets with real utility. A character or weapon that can be used, traded, or rented has value derived from gameplay. This is arguably the most durable use case.

### Provenance and Creator Royalties
NFTs can encode royalties directly — every time an NFT resells on a supporting marketplace, the creator automatically receives a percentage. This was a revolutionary model for digital artists.

### Speculation
Frankly, a significant portion of NFT value during the boom was speculative — buying with the expectation of selling to someone at a higher price.

## Types of NFTs

### Digital Art
The original high-profile use case. Artists like Beeple sold works for millions. The value derives from artistic merit, creator reputation, and collector demand.

### Profile Picture (PFP) Collections
Collections like CryptoPunks, Bored Ape Yacht Club, and Azuki focused on community and status. Each item is a variation with different traits and rarity levels.

### Gaming NFTs
In-game items represented as NFTs: characters, weapons, land, vehicles. Players own their items and can trade them outside the game. Projects like Axie Infinity and more recent titles like Illuvium use this model.

### Music NFTs
Artists release music, stems, or exclusive experiences as NFTs. Platforms like Sound.xyz let artists sell directly to fans and build community ownership around their work.

### Real World Asset (RWA) NFTs
Tokens representing ownership of physical assets: real estate, collectibles, fine wine, luxury goods. This category has grown significantly as a bridge between blockchain and traditional assets.

### Collectibles
Digital trading cards, stamps, sports moments (NBA Top Shot), and branded collectibles have established markets.

## How to Buy an NFT

1. **Set up a wallet:** MetaMask for Ethereum-based NFTs; Phantom for Solana NFTs
2. **Fund your wallet:** Add ETH, SOL, or the relevant network's native token
3. **Choose a marketplace:**
   - Ethereum: OpenSea, Blur, Foundation, SuperRare
   - Solana: Magic Eden, Tensor
4. **Browse or search** for the collection you want
5. **Buy:** Click "Buy Now" for listed items, or place a bid for auction items
6. **Confirm the transaction** in your wallet

After purchase, the NFT appears in your wallet and is visible on the marketplace under your profile.

## How to Sell an NFT

1. Connect your wallet to the marketplace where the NFT's collection is listed
2. Go to your profile → your NFTs
3. Click the NFT → List for sale
4. Set a price and duration
5. Approve the listing transaction (small gas fee)
6. When sold, proceeds minus marketplace and creator royalty fees go to your wallet

## Are NFTs Still Relevant in 2026?

The speculative PFP boom has largely faded. Most 2021-2022 collections have lost 90%+ of their peak value. However, gaming NFTs and real-world asset tokenization have continued to grow with genuine utility backing. The technology itself — unique on-chain ownership — remains foundational and is finding new applications beyond digital art speculation.

NFTs are a tool. Like any tool, their value depends entirely on what they're used for.
