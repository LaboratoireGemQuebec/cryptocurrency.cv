"What's the market cap?" is one of the first questions experienced crypto investors ask about any new project. Market capitalization is a fundamental metric for comparing assets, assessing relative size, and evaluating whether a project is over or undervalued relative to peers.

But market cap in crypto is also frequently misunderstood, manipulated, and misapplied. This guide explains how to read it correctly. For market data and analysis, visit [Crypto Vision News](/).

## What Is Market Cap?

Market capitalization is the total value of all tokens currently in circulation:

**Market Cap = Current Price × Circulating Supply**

**Example:**
- Bitcoin price: $85,000
- Circulating supply: 19.8 million BTC
- Market cap = $85,000 × 19,800,000 = $1.683 trillion

This measures the total economic value currently ascribed to the asset — similar to how stock market cap measures the total value of a company's outstanding shares.

## Circulating Supply vs Total Supply vs Maximum Supply

These three supply figures each tell a different story:

### Circulating Supply

The number of tokens currently in existence and available for trading. This is what the standard market cap calculation uses. However, tokens can be:
- Locked in smart contracts
- Vested (locked for team/investor release schedules)
- Burned (permanently removed)
- Staked (locked temporarily)

The circulating supply figure should ideally reflect only freely tradeable tokens, but data sources vary in how they handle locked tokens.

### Total Supply

All tokens created, including those locked or vested but not yet circulating. Sometimes used for a more comprehensive supply picture.

### Maximum Supply

The hard cap on how many tokens will ever exist. Bitcoin has a maximum supply of 21 million — no more will ever be created after mining completes around 2140. Not all tokens have a maximum supply; Ethereum, for instance, has no hard cap (though EIP-1559 burning offsets much of the issuance).

## Fully Diluted Valuation (FDV)

Fully Diluted Valuation calculates market cap as if all tokens (including unvested, locked, and future emissions) were in circulation today:

**FDV = Current Price × Maximum Supply**

**Why FDV matters:** Many crypto projects launch with only a small fraction of total supply in circulation. A token with $100 million market cap but a $5 billion FDV is essentially priced as if it were a $5 billion project once all tokens unlock.

**Example of why this matters:**
- Token X: Price $1, circulating supply 10 million, market cap $10 million
- Token X: Total supply 10 billion (1000x more tokens to come)
- FDV: $10 billion

Buying this token at "$10 million market cap" is actually pricing it at a $10 billion fully diluted value. As new supply unlocks, it creates constant selling pressure that suppresses price.

**Rule of thumb:** Be very cautious of tokens where FDV is more than 5–10× market cap. The unlocking schedule represents future selling pressure.

## Market Cap Categories

### Mega Cap (>$100 billion)

Bitcoin and Ethereum in most market conditions. These are the most liquid, most regulated, most institutionally held assets. Lower risk relative to the rest of crypto. Less upside percentage-wise in bull markets.

### Large Cap ($10B–$100B)

Established projects with significant adoption: Solana, BNB, XRP, Cardano, Avalanche. More volatile than mega caps but have track records spanning multiple cycles.

### Mid Cap ($1B–$10B)

Protocols with meaningful adoption but higher risk: DeFi governance tokens, L2s, established infrastructure projects. Can generate 5–20× returns in bull markets; can lose 90%+ in bears.

### Small Cap ($100M–$1B)

Earlier-stage projects, niche protocols, newer L1s. Higher risk and volatility; potential for large gains or near-total loss.

### Micro Cap (<$100M)

Early-stage, speculative, or declining projects. High probability of significant loss; occasional extreme gains. Only suitable as small speculative allocations.

## Common Market Cap Traps

### The "Low Market Cap = More Room to Grow" Fallacy

Many investors assume a $50 million market cap token can "easily 100x" while Bitcoin cannot. This ignores that:

1. Bitcoin at a $100 billion market cap has proven adoption; the $50 million token may have none
2. Market cap is a function of price × supply — tokens can be low-cap because they have no fundamental value
3. Small caps have failed far more often than they have produced large returns

Comparing market caps is useful for comparing similar projects within a category. It is not useful for predicting absolute returns.

### Ghost Market Cap

Sometimes tokens show high market cap because a small number of tokens are trading at high prices — but there is no liquidity to sell at those prices. A token with 100 tokens in circulation, each "priced" at $10 million based on one trade, shows a $1 billion market cap. This is meaningless.

Always check trading volume alongside market cap. A healthy project has volume of at least 1–5% of market cap daily.

### Inflated Circulating Supply Figures

Some projects classify all team/investor tokens as "circulating" even when they are still locked. Always verify supply figures against the project's token unlock schedule and verify on Messari, CoinGecko, or CoinMarketCap.

Bitcoin at $1.7T vs gold at $15T suggests significant potential upside if Bitcoin captures more of gold's store-of-value market share. These comparisons frame investment theses without predicting exact outcomes.

## Beyond Market Cap: Other Valuation Metrics

Market cap alone has significant limitations. Supplement it with:

- **Price/Fees ratio:** Token market cap / protocol annualized fees (like P/E for DeFi)
- **TVL ratio:** Market cap / Total Value Locked (for DeFi protocols)
- **Revenue growth:** Is actual usage growing or declining?
- **Active addresses:** Real user activity on-chain
- **Developer activity:** GitHub commits, ecosystem growth

For DeFi protocols especially, a low market cap relative to fees (P/F ratio) is a more meaningful valuation indicator than market cap alone.

Market cap is a starting point, not a conclusion. Use it to orient yourself, then dig into fundamentals to understand what that value reflects.
