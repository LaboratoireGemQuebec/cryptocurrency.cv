In 2023, the crypto industry stumbled onto something: users would do almost anything for the chance to earn airdropped tokens. Points programs — protocol-controlled scores that track user activity and (sometimes) convert to token allocations — became one of the dominant user acquisition mechanisms in DeFi. Understanding how they work is essential for anyone trying to maximize returns from protocol participation.

## The Origin of Points: Blur and Hyperliquid

The modern crypto points meta was pioneered by two projects in 2022-2023.

**Blur** launched its NFT marketplace in October 2022 with an aggressive points program. Users who listed NFTs, bid, and traded on Blur earned "Blur Points" that would convert to BLUR tokens at TGE (Token Generation Event). The airdrop was massive — Blur distributed hundreds of millions of dollars in BLUR tokens, with the most active users earning extraordinary returns. The market structure effect was dramatic: Blur quickly surpassed OpenSea in trading volume by purely financially incentivizing professional NFT traders.

**Hyperliquid** refined the model for DeFi trading. The perpetuals exchange awarded points to traders based on volume and trading activity. When HYPE finally launched in November 2024, the airdrop was one of the largest in history — completely fair-launched with no VC allocation, making it enormously popular with the community. Early Hyperliquid traders who earned maximum points received allocations worth tens to hundreds of thousands of dollars.

These events created a template that dozens of protocols copied.

## How Points Programs Work

Points programs generally follow this structure:

### Earning Points

Users earn points by taking specific on-protocol actions:
- **Depositing funds** (weighted by amount and duration)
- **Trading volume** (usually weighted by fees paid)
- **Providing liquidity** to specific pools
- **Referring new users** (referral bonuses)
- **Completing specific quests or tasks** (daily check-ins, specific transaction types)

Points are tracked off-chain by the protocol team, with blockchain activity providing the verifiable underlying data.

### Multipliers and Boosts

Most points programs include multiplier mechanics to incentivize specific behaviors:
- Early depositors earn 2-5x multipliers
- Holding a protocol's existing NFT earns a permanent boost
- Larger deposits earn higher rates per dollar
- Referring active users earns a percentage of their points

### Conversion to Tokens

When the protocol launches its token (TGE), some or all of the accumulated points convert to token allocations. The conversion ratio may be:
- Fixed (X points = Y tokens)
- Relative (your percentage of total points = your percentage of airdrop pool)
- Discretionary (the team decides allocation with points as one input)

Critically: points are not a guarantee of any token allocation. Protocols can change conversion rates, add new users retroactively, or decide not to airdrop at all. Read the fine print.

## Major Active Points Programs (Early 2026)

The points landscape changes rapidly, but here are illustrative categories of what exists in early 2026:

**DeFi infrastructure protocols:** Layer 2 networks, bridges, and cross-chain infrastructure often run long-duration points programs as they build toward token launches. Users who bridge early and maintain activity through testnet and mainnet phases typically accumulate the most points.

**Perpetuals exchanges:** Following Hyperliquid's template, multiple on-chain perpetuals platforms run trader points programs. Activity in terms of trading volume and open interest duration drive allocations.

**Lending protocols:** New lending markets offer points for depositing collateral, maintaining healthy positions, and borrowing. Some programs specifically incentivize bringing "fresh" capital (assets not already in competing protocols).

**Restaking protocols:** EigenLayer points were the most heavily farmed in 2024, with users maximizing AVS participation to earn maximum points before EIGEN launched.

## Points Farming Strategies

### Basic: Single Protocol Participation

The simplest approach — deposit funds into a protocol with a points program and wait. No active management required. Suitable for users who want exposure without monitoring.

### Intermediate: Capital Efficiency Optimization

More active farmers:
- Maximize the dollar-per-point ratio by choosing the highest-multiplier strategies
- Rotate capital to protocols with active programs and exit after TGE
- Stack multiple points programs simultaneously by using assets that earn points in multiple places (e.g., LRT tokens that themselves earn restaking points while being deposited into DeFi protocols for additional points)

### Advanced: Points Stack Building

The most sophisticated approach involves building "points stacks" — layered positions where a single dollar of capital earns points from multiple protocols simultaneously.

Example stack: Deposit ETH → Receive liquid staking token (LST points) → Restake LST via EigenLayer (restaking points) → Deposit restaking receipt token in lending protocol (lending protocol points) → Use borrowed stablecoins in another protocol (yet more points).

Each layer adds incremental risk — and multiplies the points earned.

## Risks of Points Farming

### No Guarantee of Airdrop

This cannot be overstated. Points are a marketing mechanism, not a legal commitment. Protocols have:
- Changed rules mid-campaign unfavorably
- Added new user categories that diluted early farmer allocations
- Reduced total airdrop sizes after accumulating TVL
- Launched tokens at low valuations that made farming unprofitable after gas costs

### Smart Contract Risk

Points farming often involves depositing in newer, less-audited protocols that launched specifically to ride the points meta. These carry higher smart contract exploit risk than established protocols.

### Leverage and Liquidation Risk

Complex points stacks often involve borrowed positions. If collateral values drop, stacked positions can be liquidated in sequence, causing compounded losses.

### Capital Lock-Up

Some points programs require assets to remain deposited for extended periods. If you need your capital back during a lock-up period, you may forfeit points.

### Gas Costs on Ethereum

On Ethereum mainnet, the cost of managing complex stacked positions can easily exceed the value of points earned for smaller accounts. Most sophisticated farming happens on L2s with lower fees.

## Tax Implications

Points themselves are generally not taxable events — they're more like airline miles (though tax authorities are still developing guidance). However:

- **When points convert to tokens at TGE:** This is a taxable event in most jurisdictions — ordinary income tax on the value received
- **When you sell airdropped tokens:** Capital gains tax on appreciation since receipt
- **Farming activity:** Any swaps, deposits, and withdrawals may be taxable events depending on your jurisdiction

Document all farming activity meticulously. Crypto tax software like Koinly, TaxBit, and Coinledger can import transaction histories and calculate obligations, but points-based airdrops often require manual entry.

## Is Points Farming Worth It?

The honest answer depends on your situation:

**Favorable if:** You have idle capital already deployed in DeFi, transaction costs are low (L2), you're positioned for the long term anyway, and you can participate without taking on additional risk.

**Less favorable if:** You're moving capital specifically for points (opportunity cost), you're adding significant leverage or risk, or you're paying high gas fees on L1.

The largest rewards go to early participants in successful protocols and those who take calculated risks on smaller protocols that succeed. The median outcome for systematic points farming is modest positive returns when you account for time, risk, and opportunity cost. The distribution has very high variance.
